Here is the product architecture breakdown for the Shelby WhatsApp Lead & Ordering Engine, structured as a text-based "presentation deck" progressing from the macro-level down to the micro-level. This is designed to give you, the business owner, a complete, surgically precise understanding of the system's logic, practical implementation, and safety nets.
Deck 1: The Macro Level (End-to-End UX Journey)
This level provides the 10,000-foot view of how a customer interacts with the system from arrival to fulfillment.
Phase 1: Initiation & Triage
Trigger: Customer scans a QR code (at the counter/sidewalk or table) or clicks an Instagram link
.
Greeting: The WhatsApp bot instantly greets the customer and presents a structured main menu with four quick actions
.
Branching: The customer selects their intent:
Path A: Ask a Question (FAQ)
.
Path B: Order Pickup
.
Path C: Dine-in Assist (Table Ordering)
.
Path D: Check Order Status
.
Phase 2: Core Processing
If Path A (FAQ): The bot queries the curated Shelby knowledge base to provide instant answers
.
If Path B/C (Ordering): The customer navigates categories, builds a cart, and completes a checkout flow (generating an order ID and ETA)
.
If Path D (Status): The bot fetches live updates based on the customer's phone number or order ID
.
Phase 3: Fulfillment & Handoff
Staff Dashboard Sync: All confirmed orders and manual handoff requests instantly appear on the real-time staff dashboard inside the kiosk
.
Order Lifecycle: Staff moves the order from New → Accepted → Preparing → Ready
.
Customer Notifications: The customer receives automated WhatsApp pings as the order progresses, stepping up to the window only when notified
.
Architect’s Review (Level 1): The macro flow is clean, but a critical business insight is that this system runs concurrently with physical walk-ins. To prevent a bottleneck, the architecture must ensure that the digital queue and the physical queue merge seamlessly at the fulfillment window. We achieve this by forcing all WhatsApp orders to output a simple, standardized "Order ID" that matches the counter's naming convention.

Deck 2: The Mid Level (Component Breakdown)
Zooming in, let's break down the inner workings of the core branches identified in Deck 1.
Component 1: The AI FAQ Engine (Path A Breakdown)
Step 1: Customer types an unstructured message (e.g., "Do you have vegan milk?").
Step 2 (Retrieval): The system searches only the approved Shelby FAQ documents
. It does not "guess" answers from the open internet
.
Step 3 (Evaluation):
High Confidence: Bot replies concisely (e.g., "Yes, we offer oat milk for an extra ₹50").
Low Confidence: Bot triggers a graceful fallback (e.g., "Let me connect you to our staff") and pings the staff dashboard for manual takeover
.
Component 2: The Ordering Engine (Paths B & C Breakdown)
Step 1 (Menu Browse): Bot presents active categories (e.g., Signature Coffee, Aesthetic Menu, Bakery)
.
Step 2 (Item Selection & Modifiers): Customer selects a "Blue Dopamine"
. System checks the database for required modifiers (e.g., Ice level, sweetness) and enforces valid combinations
.
Step 3 (Cart Construction): The system deterministically calculates the subtotal, taxes, and total price
. No AI is used for pricing to guarantee accuracy
.
Step 4 (Identity & Location):
Pickup: System asks for Name and Phone Number confirmation
.
Dine-in: System asks for Table Number (if not auto-captured by the QR code)
.
Step 5 (Checkout & Payment): The system generates the Order ID, estimates prep time, and provides an optional Razorpay payment link or a "Pay at Counter" cash option
.
Component 3: The Real-Time Staff Dashboard
Step 1 (Ingestion): Using Supabase real-time syncing, the order pops up on the barista's tablet in under 2 seconds
.
Step 2 (Control Panel): The dashboard features a Kanban board (New, Accepted, Preparing, Ready)
.
Step 3 (Menu Management): Includes a crucial "Availability Toggle." If the Korean Buns sell out, a barista clicks "Out of Stock," instantly removing it from the WhatsApp menu to prevent bad orders
.
Architect’s Review (Level 2): The separation of the "AI FAQ Engine" from the "Ordering Engine" is a deliberate safety measure. By using rigid, deterministic rules for ordering and pricing, we eliminate the risk of an AI hallucinating a wrong price or promising an item that isn't available
. The staff's ability to instantly toggle menu items directly addresses the "menu drift" reality of a high-velocity cafe
.

Deck 3: The Micro Level (Edge Cases & Safety Nets)
This is where we map out the "unhappy paths" and blockers. How does the system behave when things go wrong?
Micro-Flow 1: Payment Failures & Drop-offs
The Risk: A customer builds a cart, clicks the Razorpay link, but their UPI app crashes or network drops. The order sits in limbo.
The Micro-Logic:
The state machine sets the order status to ordering_payment_pending
.
If the Razorpay webhook does not return a "success" ping within 5 minutes, the bot sends an automated nudge: "Having trouble paying? You can confirm your order now and just pay cash at the counter!"
.
Business Value: You never lose a lead due to technical payment friction.
Micro-Flow 2: Peak Hour Kitchen Congestion (The "ETA Problem")
The Risk: The bot automatically tells a customer their order will take 10 minutes, but the counter is slammed and it actually takes 25 minutes, creating "The Great Disappointment"
.
The Micro-Logic:
The ETA calculation is not static.
If the staff dashboard has more than 15 orders in the Preparing column, the system automatically inflates the ETA sent to new customers (e.g., "We are experiencing a rush! Estimated prep time: 20-25 minutes").
Business Value: Manages expectations proactively, preserving your brand's reputation.
Micro-Flow 3: Concurrent "Out of Stock" Collision
The Risk: Customer A is mid-way through ordering a Hazelnut Cold Coffee. Meanwhile, the barista just poured the last drop of syrup and toggled the item to "Out of Stock" on the dashboard.
The Micro-Logic:
When Customer A clicks "Confirm Cart", the webhook does a final micro-second check against the active database
.
The database rejects the cart.
The bot replies: "Oh no! The Hazelnut Cold Coffee literally just sold out. We’ve removed it from your cart. Would you like our Shelby Signature instead?"
Business Value: Prevents refunds, counter arguments, and barista stress.
Micro-Flow 4: The "Lost Chat" (State Machine Timeout)
The Risk: A customer scans the QR, looks at the menu, puts their phone in their pocket, and tries to order 3 hours later.
The Micro-Logic:
The session state has a timestamp (last_message_at)
.
If a session is dormant for >30 minutes, the bot clears the context
. When the user replies, it restarts gracefully: "Welcome back to Shelby! Would you like to start a new order?"
Architect’s Review (Level 3): These micro-level safety nets are what transform a "cool tech demo" into a robust business tool. By prioritizing idempotency (ensuring duplicate button clicks don't charge the user twice) and graceful degradation (falling back to human staff or cash payments when tech fails), we ensure the cafe operations never come to a halt because of a software glitch
. Launching first with Pay-at-Counter and Pick-up limits the blast radius of any initial teething issues
.

Here is how the architecture and operational plan address these specific, real-world scenarios at Shelby. As a Product Architect, I look at these not just as software problems, but as hybrid physical-digital ("phygital") workflows.
1. Handling "No WhatsApp", Digital/Internet Failures, and Cash Payments
The Physical Bypass: The digital system is designed to run parallel to your physical counter, not replace it. The PRD explicitly scopes out "full POS integration" for the MVP, meaning walk-in customers without WhatsApp, or those who simply prefer talking to a human, can still walk up to the counter and order the traditional way
.
Payment Failures & Cash Orders: To minimize payment friction, the system is actively designed to support a "cash at counter" option alongside optional Razorpay digital links
. In fact, the PRD recommends launching the pilot only with the pay-at-counter option to keep operations simple for the staff initially
. If a customer's UPI fails, the bot will gracefully suggest they complete the digital order and just pay cash at the window.
Total Internet Failure: If the internet drops completely and the staff dashboard disconnects, the business does not halt. Because Shelby operates a lean, "hole-in-the-wall" model
, staff immediately revert to taking verbal orders at the window. The system's MVP architecture is lightweight enough that a temporary internet drop only pauses new digital inbound orders, without freezing the physical coffee-making process
.
2. Managing Batch Preparation (Tea vs. Coffee) During Rushes
Dashboard Visibility over Rigid AI: The system does not use AI to force a strict, rigid sequencing on your staff. Instead, it drops confirmed digital orders into a Kanban-style staff dashboard with a column specifically for "Preparing"
.
The Assembly Line Advantage: Because Shelby uses 4-5 staff members in a tiny space to create an "assembly line" (one person frothing, one pulling shots, one on buns)
, the staff can look at the "Preparing" dashboard and see the aggregate demand. If there are 5 teas scattered across 3 different orders, your tea master can brew them all at once. The dashboard acts as an information radiator, empowering your staff to batch-prep efficiently rather than forcing them to make one entire order before starting the ingredients for the next.
Prep Time Data: The database stores prep_time_min for every individual menu item
, allowing the system to accurately calculate the overall ETA of a mixed cart even if staff are batching items behind the scenes.
3. Multi-Item Orders and the "First-Come, First-Serve" Promise
Order-Level Status Tracking: To ensure customers receive their entire order at once, the system's state machine tracks fulfillment at the Order Level, not the item level
. The statuses are: new → accepted → preparing → ready
.
Staged Handoffs: The bot only sends the "Your order is ready! Please step up to the window" notification when the staff clicks "Ready" for the entire completed order on their dashboard
. This prevents the customer from crowding the window for a coffee while waiting another 5 minutes for their Korean Bun
. It preserves your 90% FCFS policy because digital customers stay out of the physical queue until their complete bundle is assembled.
4. Ensuring Excellent Service During the Weekend Rush
Shifting the Bottleneck: The PRD identifies that the "kitchen is the real bottleneck", not necessarily order taking
. By shifting 20% of peak-hour orders to WhatsApp
 and using the AI bot to instantly answer 70% of repetitive questions (like "Do you have vegan milk?")
, you physically remove the order-taking bottleneck at the counter.
Dynamic Expectations: As discussed previously, if the dashboard fills up with 20 concurrent orders, the automated ETA will inflate. Customers won't experience "The Great Disappointment"
 of a surprise 25-minute wait, because the bot sets accurate expectations before they finalize the order.
5. Additional Edge Cases & Risks You Must Prepare For
The "Fake Order" Risk (Cash-at-Counter Vulnerability): Because the system allows "pay at counter" via WhatsApp
, a malicious user could order 15 coffees digitally, never show up, and cost you inventory.
Architect's Fix: We need to implement a cart-size limit for unpaid WhatsApp orders (e.g., maximum ₹200). Anything above that requires digital payment upfront.
The Rain/Weather Protocol: Shelby is highly susceptible to Bengaluru's unpredictable rains because it relies on the sidewalk as a "Fourth Space"
.
Architect's Fix: The AI FAQ engine
 must be loaded with a "Weather Protocol" response. When it rains, the bot can instantly broadcast or reply to inquiries with: "It's raining, but our window is open! Grab an umbrella or order from your car nearby."
Concurrent "Out of Stock" Collisions: As highlighted in our previous component breakdown, menu drift is a major risk
. If a barista runs out of Hazelnut syrup, they must toggle it off in the dashboard immediately
. If a customer has it in their digital cart, the final webhook validation will strip it out before order confirmation to prevent a fulfillment crisis
